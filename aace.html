Where does the time go?

The hidden tax: data capture, transformation & reconciliation

↓ Manual reconciliation

Comparing field progress vs schedule vs cost systems. Copy-paste, format conversion, exception-hunting across Excel files.

↓ Narrative generation

Writing weekly reports, risk summaries, change impact explanations. Repetitive interpretation of the same patterns.

↓ Quality enforcement

Checking for logic errors, open ends, missing links, unrealistic durations. Should be automated, is mostly manual.

↓ Multi-stakeholder translation

Converting CPM data into language PMs, superintendents, and owners understand. Same data, different views.

The AI opportunity in project controls

LLMs excel at exactly what drains project controls teams

↑ What LLMs do well

Parse unstructured text (emails, RFIs), generate summaries, explain changes in plain language, compare versions, flag anomalies, create role-specific reports.

↓ What LLMs need help with

Domain-specific reasoning, CPM calculations, enforcing business rules, maintaining consistency. This is where ontologies matter.

The key insight

LLMs + structured ontologies + built-in workflows = reliable automation. The LLM handles language tasks; the ontology provides domain logic; workflows ensure governance. Together, they can automate what no human should spend time on.

Why now?

LLMs reached production quality in 2023–24. Construction ontologies exist (WBS, cost codes, risk taxonomies). The gap is integration + trust.

Why "just add AI" fails

Strong ontologies make AI reliable in project controls

↑ Schedule ontology

Activities, milestones, WBS hierarchy, logic types, constraints, calendars. A shared language that AI can reason about correctly.

− Risk ontology

Risk categories, impact scales, probability definitions, mitigation types, ownership structures. Enables automated risk tagging and routing.

↑ Cost/change ontology

Change types, approval workflows, cost code mappings, contract relationships. Links schedule impacts to financial consequences.

↑ Stakeholder ontology

Roles, permissions, information needs, escalation paths. AI generates right content for right audience automatically.

Ontology = guardrails LLM + ontology: "Delay in foundation pour" → WBS: 03.01.02 (Foundations) → Risk: R-047 (weather-related) → Impact: Critical path +3d → Owner: Site Super + PM → Action: Recovery plan needed LLM alone: "There might be a delay..." (no structure, no routing)

Why AACE practitioners should care

Ontologies align with AACE recommended practices: structured risk registers, WBS standards, change management protocols. AI amplifies what you already do.

From AI output to action

Built-in workflows turn AI suggestions into governed actions

↑ AI-triggered workflows

Schedule signals (float erosion, compression, critical path changes) → automatic risk entry → routed to responsible parties → tracked to closure.

+ Human-in-the-loop

AI drafts, humans approve. LLM generates risk narrative; PM validates before it hits the register. Maintain accountability, reduce labor.

↑ Role-based views

Superintendent sees field actions. PM sees cross-trade impacts. Owner sees milestone risks. Same data, different workflows, all automated.

↑ Audit trail

Every AI suggestion, every human decision, every action — logged. Supports claims, disputes, lessons learned.

The human side of automation

AI agents simplify interaction across roles

↑ Field → structured data

Superintendent texts: "Foundation pour delayed 2 days, weather." AI: Creates schedule update, risk entry, notifies PM. No training needed.

↑ Email → action items

AI parses RFI, change order, or delay notice. Extracts dates, impacts, owners. Routes to workflow. Scheduler reviews, not re-enters.

↑ Questions → answers

"What's driving the August milestone delay?" AI: Queries schedule model, assembles narrative, cites sources. PM gets insight in seconds.

↑ Mobile-first capture

Voice notes, photos with context, quick forms. AI handles the translation to structured project data.

Final thoughts · The other side

Real risks we need to address before scaling AI in project controls

↓ Security

Project data is sensitive: costs, delays, risks, contractual exposure. LLM platforms raise questions about data residency, model training, and third-party access. Obvious but non-trivial.

− Governance

Who validates AI outputs before they become official records? What's the approval chain? How do you audit what the AI suggested vs. what humans accepted?

↓ Accountability erosion

Observed behavior: when agents propose actions, humans take less ownership. "The agent did it" becomes an excuse. Guardrails don't solve the psychological shift.

− Robustness vs. flexibility

Workflows must be robust enough to enforce governance but flexible enough to handle exceptions. This balance is hard to configure and harder to maintain.

Key tension
Automation ↔ Accountability
−
The accountability gap Before AI: Human decides → Human owns With AI: Agent proposes → Human approves? → "Agent did it" mindset → Reduced scrutiny → Diffused responsibility

No easy answers

These aren't solvable by features alone. They require organizational design, clear policies, and continuous reinforcement of human accountability.
